Explorations

Places

Each place gives me something and stays with me.

I've been deliberate about not letting any single heritage become the default lens. The more different the context, the more it shakes loose what I think I know. Here's a sampling.

Stepanakert, Nagorno-Karabakh
A territory that no longer exists. A place frozen in time. Stayed with an elderly couple — metal bed, Soviet-era refrigerator, untouched since the 70s. Set out for a ghost town with a few equally nutty travellers — police turned us back. Drank strong local spirits with a soldier and a tomato seller anyway, strangers who became friends for an afternoon.
Zhongshan, China
The scale hits you first — malls, apartment complexes, factories, infrastructure, all immaculate and enormous. But enmeshed in that is a culture that doesn't announce itself. I was there sourcing prototypes. The people I worked with knew things I didn't — not theoretically, but through repetition, volume, and years of making things for the world.
Auroville, India
An entire town built from values rather than economics. The place has a real intensity: mud roads, no loud signboards, event schedules you only know if you're plugged in. Sustainable architecture, excellent food options, sound healing, spas, artisan-led workshops, small manufacturing facilities — a serious experiment running through everything. The intensity is almost cult-like, and I mean that as a compliment. I've been twice.
The Carpathians, Ukraine
I never imagined I'd be in the Carpathians, volunteering for an ecstatic dance festival before anything existed. Just mountains, pine trees, silence, tents, and stacks of beams waiting to become yurts, domes, kitchens, and gathering spaces. We dug the first holes, foraged mushrooms, drank tea from wild herbs, and talked through what the place could become.
Kyoto, Japan
An evening walk along the Philosopher's Path, then Yamazaki single malt at sundown. Kiyomizu-dera. Fushimi Inari-taisha. Everything in Kyoto feels considered — like the city made a decision about what matters and quietly removed the rest.
San Francisco, USA
A founder conversation in Lafayette Park, an event at the Presidio, investor meetings, a leisurely walk by the Embarcadero — San Francisco makes ambition feel strangely ordinary. The setting helps: hills, sea, shifting light, and a certain openness that makes the city feel both intense and calm.
Istanbul, Turkey
Istanbul feels layered from the first hour — Galata Tower, the Grand Bazaar, Hürrem Sultan Hammam, mezes, stories, the Bosphorus cutting between continents. History there doesn't sit in the background. It presses in.
Svaneti, Georgia
Four days trekking the mountains, stopping in villages, eating whatever the hosts put on the table — grown on those pristine slopes. A kid at one of the guesthouses who showed us magic tricks. An Israeli brother and sister talking about their military service over dinner. Oh, the characters we met along the way.
Ubud, Bali
Stayed in a wooden hut at a Russian-run spa retreat in the jungle above Ubud. Days on a scooty through rice terraces and village roads. Evenings, the place ran its own programme: tea, bonfire, ecstatic dance, cacao, sauna, flower-petal pools.
Menorca, Spain
Walked the island end to end. Slept by the sea, stared at the stars, snorkelled when the coast allowed. No agenda — just movement and stillness in turns.
Stages

Daunting and full of possibility.

Wrote and acted in plays from school days through the engineering years and the first job. Tried improv. The high came from the creation as much as the performance.

Now mostly in the audience. Theatre that's serious, political, or literary — G5A, Prithvi, NCPA. Comedy with teeth. Live music in smaller rooms — fifty to three hundred people, nice and groovy.

Kitchen

First principles, not rules.

I don't follow recipes so much as reason through them — mutton in many variations, ramen built from whatever's in the kitchen, baking and grilling, kept simple and done well. Dishes I understand, not dishes I've perfected by repetition.

The eating works the same way. I order the thing I don't recognise. Curiosity first, always. But the loyalties run deep too — litti chokha non-negotiable in Patna, Hyderabadi mutton biryani, a good sandwich (that takes me to Antwerp), momos done right.

Practice

The slow side. Daily attention to living systems.

Fermentation is where it goes deepest. It started professionally — my team at Godrej Appliances was building a fermentation device, I got curious, then went deep. Kanji, kombucha, kefir, ginger bug, sauerkraut, jalapeños in brine. The kefir's been continuous for nearly a year. Every few days: sieve the grains, clean the vessel, start the next batch. Same rhythm with the kombucha SCOBY when I had it.

The garden runs the same logic, made spatial. Brahmi, tulsi, neem, mint, rosemary, New Zealand spinach.

About

I grew up in a small town, went to a Ramakrishna Mission boarding school, and learned early that the world was mine to figure out.

Engineering at Delhi College of Engineering, then strategic design at TU Delft. Delft cracked something open.

I've built innovation studios inside conglomerates and a Web3 startup from a Paris apartment. Raised through crypto winters. Shipped products people actually used. Walked away when the chapter was done.

Lived in Delft, The Hague, Antwerp, Barcelona, Paris. Back in Mumbai now — with Subhasree and our two kids.

Most days now: kefir grains kept alive, herbs on the verandah, two small humans, food cooked slow.

I'm drawn to how people live — their rituals, systems, aspirations, and contradictions. The next thing I build will probably sit somewhere between culture, capital, and emerging technology.
